## Overview

Phi Am (Thai: ผีอำ) is a spirit in Thai folklore that visits sleepers at night and presses down on the chest, immobilizing the victim and obstructing breathing. The phenomenon is part of a global family of sleep-paralysis traditions — analogous to the Old Hag of Newfoundland, the Mara of Scandinavia, kanashibari in Japan, bangungot in the Philippines, and khmaoch sangkat in Cambodia — in which a supernatural figure is held responsible for the immobility and dread that accompanies a particular sleep state. In Thailand the term has become so deeply associated with the underlying experience that 'phi am' is used by both lay speakers and medical practitioners to mean sleep paralysis. Modern sleep medicine identifies the phenomenon as a parasomnia in which atonia of REM sleep persists into wakefulness, frequently accompanied by hypnopompic hallucinations of presence, weight, and pressure.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Thai. 'Phi' (ผี) is the generic Thai word for ghost or spirit; 'am' (อำ) is a verb meaning 'to press down', 'to hold in place', or 'to suppress'.
- **Meaning**: 'The ghost that presses down' or 'the suppressing ghost', describing the felt experience of being pinned to the bed.
- **First use**: Vernacular Thai usage; documented in 20th-century Thai folklore writing and in cross-cultural sleep-paralysis literature.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Spirit attack** _(Thai folk tradition)_

Traditional interpretation: a wandering spirit (phi) takes advantage of the sleeper's vulnerable state to press down on the chest and feed on or torment them.

### Scientific

**Sleep paralysis (REM atonia intrusion)** _(Mainstream sleep medicine, Baland Jalal, Devon E. Hinton)_

A parasomnia in which the muscle atonia of REM sleep persists into early wakefulness, leaving the sleeper conscious but unable to move. Frequently accompanied by hypnopompic hallucinations of presence, pressure, and dread, which the brain interprets through culturally available imagery.

### Skeptical

**Cultural framing of a universal experience** _(Cross-cultural sleep paralysis researchers)_

Phi Am is not a distinct entity but the Thai cultural script applied to a worldwide neurological phenomenon, in the same way that the Old Hag, Mara, or kanashibari are local scripts for the same underlying experience.
